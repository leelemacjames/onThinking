The Cognitive Foundations

Assimilation vs. Accommodation

Jean Piaget identified two mechanisms by which we integrate new knowledge. One preserves our existing framework; the other transforms it. The difference matters enormously for how scientific understanding propagates through society.

Assimilation

New information is spliced into the existing framework without altering it. This works when experience aligns with our internal model, but creates a subtle danger: we may reinterpret evidence so that it appears to confirm what we already believe. The world is not permitted to correct the theory; instead the theory quietly reshapes perception.

Accommodation

The process of revising our mental representation so it aligns with new experience. Accommodation is uncomfortable: it forces us to acknowledge that our expectations were wrong. Yet it is precisely this moment of cognitive dissonance that genuine learning occurs. Teaching, at its best, provokes exactly this.

"I couldn't reduce it to the freshman level. That means we don't really understand it."

Attributed to Richard Feynman, on spin-statistics*Widely circulated but possibly apocryphal. The anecdote appears in various retellings; no primary source has been confirmed. Its pedagogical force does not depend on literal accuracy.

The point is not that physicists lack formal knowledge. The anecdote reveals something deeper: some scientific ideas can be understood structurally without admitting an intuitive explanation accessible to everyone. Knowledge sometimes outruns explanation.

The Quaternary Level

Four Kinds of Curious

At the outermost layer, participants adopt distinct epistemic postures

01

The Orthodoxy Defender

Believes the current scientific consensus should be defended against misunderstanding. Emphasises the authority of established process and reinforces consensus. Valuable as a stabilising force, but risks conflating "the current model" with "the truth."

02

The Curious Skeptic

Accepts mainstream science but probes its boundaries. Respectful of expertise yet intrigued by the possibility that recent discoveries may expose limitations in existing models. The engine of accommodation at the public level.

03

The Abstract Critic

Engages logically but rarely tests ideas against empirical reality. More interested in internal consistency of arguments than confronting them with data. Skilled at revealing hidden assumptions, but disconnected from the evidential ground.

04

The Social Constructivist

Focuses on the sociology of science rather than its empirical content. Points out that observation is no longer simple: modern data passes through layers of modelling, interpretation, and computational mediation. The model-builders are human, subject to bias.

The Pencil Problem

Science as Distributed Knowledge

No single person possesses the knowledge required to produce a simple pencil. The wood, graphite, rubber, machinery, and logistics involve countless individuals cooperating through a system none fully comprehends.

After Leonard Read, I, Pencil (popularised by Milton Friedman)

Modern science resembles this pencil. No individual mind grasps the whole structure. Knowledge is distributed across institutions, disciplines, and generations. This isn't a defect; it's what collective knowing looks like at scale.

The New Layer

This Is Where LLMs Come In

If knowledge is already distributed beyond any single mind, and if each interpretive layer adds bias and simplification, then the arrival of large language models introduces a genuinely novel question:

What happens when the interpreter is not human?

LLMs operate across all four levels simultaneously. They consume primary literature, secondary analysis, tertiary review, and quaternary discourse. They assimilate everything, but do they ever accommodate?

An LLM can generate a freshman-style explanation of spin-statistics on demand, something even Feynman joked was out of reach. But does that constitute understanding, or is it the most sophisticated assimilation mechanism ever built: reshaping the appearance of knowledge without genuine cognitive dissonance?

The epistemic hierarchy gains a fifth participant: a non-human layer that compresses interpretation rather than adding it. Whether this compression preserves or destroys meaning is the open question of our moment.

And there is a further possibility. Used as an adversarial tutor, an LLM could accelerate accommodation: probing a student's model, surfacing contradictions, forcing the discomfort where genuine learning begins. The same tool that makes assimilation frictionless could, in the right pedagogical frame, become the most effective instrument for disrupting it.

The educational funnel, from school through university, was already a system for progressive refinement of approximation. LLMs now sit alongside that funnel, offering a lateral pathway that bypasses the traditional layering. The epistemic distance from evidence has been radically reconfigured.

The broader public forms the ecosystem in which science operates: questioning, interpreting, challenging, and occasionally forcing accommodation where complacent assimilation has taken hold. If science advances at all, it does so because individuals at every level remain willing to confront the uncomfortable possibility that their understanding might be wrong.
